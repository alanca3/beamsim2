# BeamSimII — Planning-Phase Research Findings: Simulating 3D Loudspeaker Radiation to Feed a Beamforming Filter Designer

## TL;DR
- **Use frequency-domain BEM as the physics engine, not analytic methods** — specifically the free, open-source NumCalc/Mesh2HRTF (Burton–Miller collocation BEM + multi-level fast multipole method) as the lowest-friction path, with COMSOL's Pressure Acoustics BEM as the higher-fidelity commercial fallback. BEM is the only method on the table that natively captures baffle diffraction, full-3D enclosure shape, driver placement timing/phase, and mutual coupling from a single CAD surface mesh — exactly what the user wants to supply.
- **The ~6-hour budget is the binding constraint and forces a hybrid/multi-resolution strategy.** A full-bandwidth (to 20 kHz) BEM solve over a whole loudspeaker enclosure runs on the order of a day on a workstation (NextGenAudio's digital-twin loudspeaker took "around a day" for 0–20 kHz, 1,850 directions, point-source drivers), because element count scales as frequency-squared and the dense problem is expensive. Realistic full-resolution BEM is tractable only up to a few kHz over a whole enclosure; above that you must use frequency-band remeshing, axisymmetric shortcuts, baffle-only meshes, sparse-frequency + spherical-harmonic interpolation, or splice in the analytic piston/diffraction model.
- **Represent the output as a per-driver complex (magnitude+phase) transfer-function tensor indexed [frequency × direction], sampled on a near-uniform sphere grid (Lebedev/Fliege/t-design), all referenced to ONE common spatial phase origin.** This is exactly the "columns of the steering matrix H" that the EUSIPCO 2024 constant-directivity beamformer (Luo, Amazon) and the AES GLL complex-summation model (Feistel et al., Paper 7254) consume. Carry full metadata (driver positions/orientations, coordinate system, observation radius, speed of sound). Target GLL/CLF balloon format for interoperability plus native HDF5/SOFA + `.frd` exports for REW/VituixCAD/MATLAB.

## Key Findings

### Method recommendation in one line
Analytic models (Rayleigh/piston + Distributed Edge Dipole diffraction) are fast and ideal for sanity checks and the high-frequency tail, but they cannot natively handle an arbitrary 3D enclosure, side/rear drivers, or mutual coupling. BEM can, directly from the exact CAD surface the user already plans to provide, and it is the established tool for this job (AKABAK/ABEC, COMSOL, the Mesh2HRTF digital-twin loudspeaker). FEM is unnecessary overhead for an exterior radiation problem (it requires a volumetric mesh of the surrounding air); FDTD/pseudospectral (k-Wave) is a time-domain niche that is awkward for fine broadband directivity and is really built for ultrasound/medical imaging. **Recommendation: BEM as primary, analytic as validation + HF supplement.**

### The data the filter designer actually needs (this drives the whole output design)
The downstream beamformer (Yuancheng Luo, *Constant Directivity Loudspeaker Beamforming*, EUSIPCO 2024, pp. 246–250; arXiv:2407.01860) is formulated entirely on a complex transfer matrix: each driver's simulated complex directional response forms the columns; covariance matrices **A** (over the "accept"/target angular region) and **R** (over the "reject"/whole-sphere region) are built by integrating those responses over the sphere; the optimal weights maximize a generalized Rayleigh quotient w·ᴴ·A·w / w·ᴴ·R·w subject to a directivity-index constraint. Luo adds a "generalized Rayleigh penalty quotient (GRPQ) G(A, R + ΓΣ, w) which adds a diagonal positive-definite penalty matrix ΓΣ to the denominator covariance matrix R," which drives each driver's weight |wₙ|→0 outside its operating range and thereby generalizes crossovers — important because BeamSimII targets heterogeneous arrays (woofer/mid/tweeter). The AES GLL complex-summation model (Feistel, Ahnert, Hughes, Olson, *Simulating the Directivity Behavior of Loudspeakers with Crossover Filters*, AES 123rd Convention, 2007, Paper 7254) does the same forward prediction by complex-summing high-resolution per-driver directional data after applying crossover/EQ filters. **Conclusion: the simulator must output per-driver complex frequency responses over a dense sphere of directions, phase-referenced consistently — nothing less will let the filter designer steer beams correctly.**

## Details

### 1. Method-by-method rundown

**Analytic / semi-analytic.**
- *Rayleigh integral / piston-in-infinite-baffle*: closed-form. A flat circular piston of radius a in an infinite baffle has directivity D(θ) = 2·J₁(ka·sinθ)/(ka·sinθ). This captures driver directivity narrowing with frequency via the ka product (k = 2π/λ, a = radius): near-omnidirectional for ka < ~0.5–1, progressively beaming as ka grows, with side lobes appearing at the Bessel-function zeros. At ka ≫ 1 the main lobe narrows to roughly ±20°. It does NOT capture finite-baffle diffraction, enclosure shape, or rear radiation. Captures: driver directivity, on-axis HF beaming. Misses: diffraction, edges, box, mutual coupling. Cost: negligible (milliseconds).
- *Baffle-diffraction models*: Vanderkooy "A Simple Theory of Cabinet Edge Diffraction" (JAES vol. 39, pp. 923–933; geometric theory of diffraction); the **Distributed Edge Dipole (DED) model of Urban, Heil, Pignon, Combet & Bauman, "The Distributed Edge Dipole (DED) Model for Cabinet Diffraction Effects," JAES vol. 52, no. 10, pp. 1043–1059, Oct. 2004** (AES e-lib id 13024), which is "developed based on the Kirchhoff approximation using distributed dipoles with their axes perpendicular to the baffle edge" and matches measurement better than Vanderkooy at large off-axis angles; and the Svensson "BTM" (edge source) method, which gives correct results at all angles/frequencies with higher-order diffraction terms. Tools: Tolvan "The Edge", the VituixCAD Diffraction tool, Jeff Bagby's tools. **Critical limitation found repeatedly: these standard algorithms are for PLANAR baffles only — they use a high-frequency geometric-optics approximation that neglects the sides and back of the enclosure. As one practitioner summary put it, "irregular sides is no problem, but 3D effects are not possible with standard algorithms." This disqualifies pure analytic diffraction for BeamSimII's arbitrary-3D requirement.**
- *Complex point-source summation (AES GLL approach)*: represents each driver/passband as a complex directional source measured/simulated at high resolution, then sums them with filters. This is a data/prediction model, not a physics solver — it is actually the downstream forward model and exactly what BeamSimII should feed.

**Boundary Element Method (BEM) — recommended.**
- Solves the Helmholtz equation on the *surface* mesh only (no air-volume mesh). For exterior radiation, the radiation condition at infinity is built into the free-space Green's function. Naturally captures: baffle diffraction, full enclosure shape, driver placement (timing/phase/path differences), and mutual coupling between drivers — because it solves the actual geometry. The diaphragm is imposed as a velocity boundary condition (normal velocity on cone elements; rigid/sound-hard elsewhere). The conventional cone boundary condition is "constant velocity on the cone with a linear reduction to zero across the surround."
- Mesh rule: ~6 elements per wavelength (industry rule for higher-order elements; 6–8 for NumCalc's constant collocation elements; there is a hard "Nyquist"-like floor near 2 elements/wavelength below which results are nonsensical). ABEC's flat zero-order elements need more elements for a given accuracy.
- Cost scaling (the crux): number of surface elements N grows as frequency-squared (fixed elements/wavelength). Classical dense BEM is O(N²) memory and O(N³) for a direct solve (O(N²) per iteration for iterative solvers). The Fast Multipole Method (FMM) reduces the matrix-vector product to ~O(N log N) and memory toward ~O(N) (one EM FMBEM study measured ~N^1.24). Multi-level FMM (used by NumCalc) takes complexity from O(N²) toward ~O(N log N). The Burton–Miller formulation is used to stabilize the solution at the "irregular/critical frequencies" where the plain surface-Helmholtz integral is non-unique.
- Element size at 20 kHz: wavelength ≈ 17 mm, so element edge ≈ 2.86 mm (6/λ) to ~3.4 mm (5/λ). COMSOL's meshing blog confirms this: "At the high-frequency end, this leads to a maximum element size, or spatial resolution, of (17 mm/5 =) 3.2 mm."

**Finite Element Method (FEM).**
- Meshes the air volume (plus PML/radiation boundary). Versatile, fully multiphysics (can do structural-acoustic cone vibration and magnet/voice-coil electromagnetics), sparse matrices, parallelizes well. But for an exterior radiation problem it is overkill: DOFs scale as roughly f³ and memory blows up. COMSOL's own training material: a car-cabin FEM model at 2000 Hz was 4.1M DOFs, 16 GB RAM, ~10 min; at 4000 Hz it required >128 GB RAM. Best used for the low-frequency interior/structural part, or in a hybrid FEM-BEM scheme (structural cone/enclosure by FEM, exterior radiation by BEM — COMSOL's Vibroacoustic Loudspeaker tutorial does exactly this).

**FDTD / pseudospectral time-domain (k-Wave, Strata-FDTD).**
- k-Wave (Treeby, Cox & Jaros; UCL/Brno) is a MATLAB/C++/Python k-space pseudospectral time-domain solver, GPU-accelerated (NVIDIA), open-source, designed for ultrasound/medical/heterogeneous-media. PSTD permits coarse grids (~2 points/wavelength) but needs a correction for time-varying source discretization. Time-domain gives a broadband impulse in one run, but a Cartesian grid over the whole domain to 20 kHz is huge, curved baffles suffer staircasing, and frequency-dependent directive sources require spherical-harmonic source encoding (Hargreaves/Sheaffer-style local SH encoding). Discontinuous-Galerkin FDTD has been validated against loudspeaker directivity measurements. Net: viable in principle, awkward and non-standard for fine broadband loudspeaker directivity; Strata-FDTD is a smaller/less-mature project. Not recommended as primary.

### 3. Driver physics modeling

- **T/S → LF response**: The small-signal parameters (fs, Qts, Qes, Qms, Vas, Re, Le, Bl, Mms, Cms, Sd) set the low-frequency rolloff and box alignment. A sealed box raises system fc and Qtc; a vented (Thiele) box gives a 4th-order high-pass alignment. COMSOL's Lumped Loudspeaker Driver tutorial feeds T/S into an Electric Circuit interface coupled to the acoustic domain and compares to the analytic flat-piston result; AKABAK does the same via LEM. This produces the cone *velocity* (the BEM boundary condition) and on-axis sensitivity.
- **Voice-coil inductance → HF rolloff**: Le alone (a plain inductor) is inaccurate because the coil is a *lossy* inductor (eddy currents/skin effect in the pole piece). Better models: **Wright** (J. R. Wright, "An Empirical Model for Loudspeaker Motor Impedance," JAES vol. 38, no. 10, pp. 749–754, 1990), **Leach** (JAES vol. 50, no. 6, pp. 442–450, 2002), **Vanderkooy semi-inductance** (impedance Z = Ks·√(jω); JAES vol. 37, no. 3, 1989), **Thorborg–Futtrup** semi-inductance + shorting (JAES vol. 59, no. 9, pp. 612–627, 2011 — the model REW uses), and the **LR2 / LR-N** ladder models. REW's model is: Re + dR, a series inductance LEB, then a parallel combination LE‖KE‖Rss, where the semi-inductance KE has impedance varying as √ω to model eddy/skin effects, and Rss models conductive material in the magnet system; impedance should be measured to 20 kHz to fit it. This HF impedance rise shapes the driver's top-octave magnitude.
- **Cone breakup**: options in increasing fidelity — (a) rigid piston (uniform cone velocity; cheapest; misses breakup and fine HF directivity); (b) an assumed/parameterized flexible velocity profile (membrane/modal shapes); (c) import a measured or assumed directivity; (d) full structural FEM of the cone giving the true vibration pattern, then structural-acoustic coupling into BEM. For BeamSimII's "few T/S params + CAD" input, full structural FEM is out of scope — **recommend a rigid-piston velocity BC (or a simple parameterized profile) as the diaphragm boundary condition**, accepting that genuine cone-breakup peaks won't be predicted from first principles. The NextGenAudio author explicitly named the simplified driver/point-source model as the weak point of his toolchain.
- **Cone size → directivity**: the ka product governs beaming via the piston directivity function — captured automatically if the cone is meshed as a finite radiating surface in BEM, or analytically via the Bessel formula. Reference rule of thumb: a source is approximately omnidirectional for ka < ~1 (e.g., the limiting frequency f = c/2a is ~550 Hz for an 8-inch radiator, ~5.5 kHz for a 1-inch dome).
- **Diaphragm as BEM boundary condition**: prescribe normal velocity over the cone elements (constant on the cone, tapering to zero across the surround); rigid/sound-hard elsewhere. Detail level: enough elements to satisfy ~6/wavelength at the top frequency you trust. Structural-acoustic coupling is only needed for true breakup; an assumed velocity profile suffices for the directivity of a well-behaved driver.

### 4. Baffle diffraction & enclosure effects

- **Significance**: edge diffraction produces the "baffle step" (the ~+6 dB transition from full-space to half-space loading as wavelength shrinks below baffle size) plus a ripple of peaks/dips. For a typical baffle this matters from roughly a few hundred Hz up through several kHz. Edge rounding matters: the NextGenAudio model (40 mm radius) noted that the rounding "significantly affects the polar and on-axis response... above approximately 1 kHz," and dropping to a 20 mm radius "resulted in notable irregularities... due to edge-reflection effects." Driver offset on the baffle shifts/scatters the ripple (the asymmetric-placement "magic numbers"). A square vs round baffle does not reduce total diffraction — it spreads it over time.
- **Analytic handling**: Vanderkooy GTD, Urban/Heil DED (dipole edge sources), Svensson BTM. Good — but planar baffles only (no true 3D box).
- **BEM handling**: captured natively and in full 3D because BEM solves on the actual enclosure surface, including edges, sides, rear, and rounding. This is the single biggest reason to prefer BEM for BeamSimII.
- **Accuracy lost if approximated**: planar analytic diffraction misses 3D enclosure effects (side/rear contributions, rounding, non-planar baffles), and the DED model misses 2nd-order diffraction (it gets the general −6 dB step and behaves well off-axis but misses second-order effects). For a directivity-controlled multi-driver speaker with side/rear drivers (the Beolab 90 / Kii Three class the user targets), planar approximations are inadequate; BEM is needed.

### 5. Simulation output / data representation (the deliverable's core)

**Recommended primary structure**: a per-driver complex tensor **H[driver, frequency, direction]** of pressure transfer functions (magnitude+phase), where "direction" indexes a near-uniform sphere sampling. This maps directly onto the beamformer's steering matrix (drivers = columns). Equivalent/companion forms:
- Per-direction impulse responses (IFFT of the complex spectra) — useful for time-of-flight and REW/convolution.
- Spherical-harmonic (SH) decomposition per frequency — compact, enables clean interpolation/rotation and direct directivity-index/covariance integrals; the spherical-array beamforming literature and Luo's "Spherical Harmonic Covariance" work operate here.
- Industry balloon (GLL/CLF) — for interoperability and validation.

**Angular sampling on the sphere**: avoid naive lat/long grids (they oversample the poles and are ill-conditioned). Use near-uniform schemes with known integration weights for clean power/DI integration — **Lebedev (~1.3·(N+1)² points for SH order N), Fliege–Maier ((N+1)² points), Gauss–Legendre, spherical t-designs, or an icosphere**. The required SH order N (hence point count) rises with frequency and source size, since directivity gets more complex toward 20 kHz. NextGenAudio used 1,850 equidistributed points for 0–20 kHz; industry balloons (GLL/CLF) use 5° resolution. **Practical recommendation: a Lebedev or Fliege grid sized to the highest SH order needed at 20 kHz (commonly a few hundred to ~2,000 points), or ≤5° if targeting GLL/CLF. The grid must over-resolve relative to the SH order the optimizer will use, to avoid spatial aliasing.**

**Frequency sampling**: BEM solves frequency-by-frequency, so frequencies are independent and total time scales ~linearly with the count. For directivity you can use a coarser frequency grid than for crossover work; log spacing or ~1/24-octave is conventional for display/smoothing, but the optimizer wants a defined linear or log bin set. HRTF practice is 0–24 kHz in 150 Hz steps (~160 lines). A phase-extrapolation study saved ~86% of compute by using ~2 bins per auditory band — evidence you can simulate on a sparse frequency grid and then interpolate (ideally in the SH/min-phase domain).

**Phase handling & reference (critical, easy to get wrong)**: ALL per-driver responses must share ONE consistent spatial phase origin, or the multi-driver sum mis-steers. In measurement this is the point-of-rotation / acoustic-center problem: if the turntable pivot ≠ the source's acoustic center, you get an artificial "egg-shaped" pattern and a phase error that grows with frequency; the fix is a complex (phase) correction. The acoustic center is "the locus of the equivalent point source that yields the same far-field pressure in magnitude and phase in a specified direction" (Trott, 1977); phase/group-delay definitions of it drift with frequency. **In simulation this is actually easier and a key advantage: define one global coordinate origin, keep every driver's complex response referenced to that single origin (do NOT minimum-phase-ify or re-zero each driver independently), and store the true time-of-flight/excess phase implied by each driver's real 3D position.** This preserves the inter-driver phase relationships the beamformer needs. You may decompose into minimum-phase + excess-phase (pure delay/time-of-flight) if useful, but keep the common origin. VituixCAD's note that "phase response of dual-channel measurement includes difference between rotation center and actual acoustical center of driver" is the real-world analog.

**Metadata that must travel with the dataset**: driver positions and orientations in 3D; the coordinate system definition and phase origin; observation radius and whether far-field is assumed; sample rate / frequency grid; speed of sound / air temperature / humidity / pressure (these set reference conditions and air attenuation); driver-area normalization; and units/reference level (e.g., SPL at 1 m / 2.83 V). EASE SpeakerLab/GLL store exactly this kind of metadata plus embedded filters.

**Interoperability with REW / VituixCAD / MATLAB**:
- *VituixCAD* reads tab/space/semicolon `.txt`/`.frd` (magnitude+phase) and `.zma` impedance; it expects per-single-driver responses (it sums them itself) with off-axis sets at matched horizontal/vertical angles, and explicitly supports importing BEM (Akabak) directivity. **Targeting VituixCAD-style `.frd` per-driver/per-angle exports is the most pragmatic interop path for the user.**
- *REW* works in impulse/FR and can import/export FR; useful for validation and EQ.
- *MATLAB* is the natural home for the H-tensor (the NextGenAudio author loaded BEM data into MATLAB to apply crossovers and least-squares cardioid filters).
- *GLL/CLF*: GLL is AFMG's proprietary high-resolution complex format created via EASE SpeakerLab (manufacturer licensing required); AFMG "recommends so-called multi-source measurements... the complex polar data of each individual driver or pass band... will be measured separately" — which aligns perfectly with BeamSimII's per-driver output. CLF is the more open balloon format used by room-acoustics tools (NextGenAudio exported BEM results to CLF via the free CLF authoring tools). **Recommendation: native HDF5/NumPy/SOFA for the working dataset, `.frd` for VituixCAD, and CLF for balloon interoperability.**

### 6. Constant-directivity / beamforming theory (context for what to provide)

- **Luo, *Constant Directivity Loudspeaker Beamforming* (EUSIPCO 2024, pp. 246–250; arXiv:2407.01860, Amazon)**: handles *heterogeneous* arrays (woofer/mid/tweeter with different operating ranges). Defines MECD (maximum efficiency constant directivity) and MSCD (maximum sensitivity constant directivity) beamformers as quadratically-constrained quadratic programs maximizing a generalized Rayleigh quotient G(A,R,w) = w·ᴴ·A·w / w·ᴴ·R·w with a generalized-directivity-index equality constraint, plus a frequency-regularization penalty (ΓΣ added to the denominator R) that smoothly crosses drivers in/out of band without explicit crossovers. **What it needs from BeamSimII: the complex per-driver directional transfer functions to build A (accept region) and R (reject/whole-sphere) covariance matrices at each frequency.**
- **AES GLL complex summation (Feistel, Ahnert, Hughes, Olson, AES Paper 7254, 123rd Convention, 2007; concept introduced in the 121st-Convention high-resolution-data paper, 2006, GLL released in EASE 4.2, March 2007)**: predicts a multi-way loudspeaker's directivity balloon by complex-summing high-resolution per-driver directional data after applying gain/delay/IIR crossover filters. The work established that complex (phase-bearing) high-resolution data is essential — magnitude-only data yields wrong combinations. **This is essentially the forward model BeamSimII feeds, and it validates the data-format choice.**
- **Keele CBT (Constant Beamwidth Transducer)**: a curved (circular-arc/spherical-cap) array with frequency-independent Legendre-function amplitude shading → constant beamwidth with very low side lobes above a cutoff. Practical implementations discretize/truncate the shading (a 3-dB stepped approximation maintained out to −12 dB preserves pattern control); straight-line/flat-panel CBTs use signal delays; "Multi-CBT" overlapped shadings give customizable directivity. Relevance: a benchmark target pattern and a check that BeamSimII can represent curved/3D layouts and reproduce a known result.
- **Sound-zone / acoustic-contrast control (Nelson, Elliott, Coleman, Doclo & Moonen)**: maximize acoustic energy in a bright zone vs a dark zone — the same mathematical machinery (energy ratios → generalized eigenproblem) and the same input need (complex transfer functions from each source to each control point). Reinforces the H-matrix output design.
- **Frame for the filter designer**: every one of these methods consumes the same thing — complex, phase-consistent, per-driver responses sampled over space and frequency. Provide that and all of them become possible.

### 7. Validation strategy (no hardware)

- **Analytic cross-check**: simulate a flat circular piston in an infinite baffle and compare to the closed-form D(θ) = 2·J₁(ka·sinθ)/(ka·sinθ) and the on-axis pressure formula; BEM and the analytic model must agree where the piston assumption holds. COMSOL's Lumped Loudspeaker Driver tutorial explicitly compares to the piston analytic solution.
- **Sphere benchmark**: radiation/scattering from a sphere has an exact spherical-harmonic solution; Mesh2HRTF/NumCalc were validated this way (the NumCalc paper benchmarks cube-based and icosahedron-based sphere meshes). Use it as a unit test.
- **Reproduce a published BEM example**: the COMSOL "Loudspeaker Radiation: BEM Acoustics" tutorial, or a Mesh2HRTF tutorial, as an end-to-end regression.
- **BEM vs analytic diffraction**: in the planar-baffle regime, BEM should match DED/Vanderkooy; divergence flags either mesh problems or genuine 3D effects.
- **Power / DI integration checks**: integrate |p|² over the sphere with the grid's quadrature weights to get radiated power and directivity index; check energy conservation and that DI → 3 dB for an omni-in-baffle at low ka (the textbook value).
- **Mesh-convergence**: refine elements/wavelength (6 → 8 → 10) and confirm results stop changing; respect the ~2-element/wavelength hard floor.
- **Error metrics**: per-frequency magnitude/phase error vs analytic, spatially-averaged dB error, DI error, and reciprocity/energy checks.

### 8. The ~6-hour budget — concrete numbers and the hybrid strategy

Benchmarks gathered (distinguishing measured figures from rules of thumb):
- Full-band HRTF/loudspeaker meshes exceed 100,000 triangular elements (Mesh2HRTF official statement). A *uniform* 20 kHz head mesh would need >1,000,000 elements (mesh-grading paper), reduced to ~18,000 only by HRTF-specific grading that concentrates resolution at the ears — a trick that does NOT fully transfer to a broadband-radiating loudspeaker enclosure, where the whole surface radiates.
- RAM at 20 kHz for a high-resolution mesh: ~8 GB (NumCalc/grading paper, 7.99 GB measured); 80k-triangle meshes use >10 GB/instance and "hours per frequency step"; the same mesh can jump from a couple of GB at 500 Hz to >20 GB at 24 kHz (Mesh2HRTF wiki). Recommended workstation RAM: 32 GB or more.
- NumCalc is single-threaded per instance and CPU-only; you parallelize by running many frequency steps as separate processes (so many cores + lots of RAM help). The default solver is iterative (conjugate-gradient-squared); a direct LU solve is only feasible for small N.
- Full 0–20 kHz loudspeaker run with 1,850 directions and point-source drivers: "around a day" on an unspecified many-core/large-RAM PC (NextGenAudio, verbatim: "the calculations took around a day to finish").
- Scaling laws: N ∝ f²; dense memory O(N²) / direct solve O(N³); FMM ~O(N log N) matvec and ~O(N) memory (measured ~N^1.24 in one study); total time ~linear in the number of frequency lines.

**Implication**: a single full-resolution, full-enclosure 20 kHz BEM solve will NOT fit in ~6 hours on a workstation. To hit the budget you need a multi-resolution / hybrid plan:
1. **Frequency-band remeshing** (coarse mesh at low f, fine only where needed) — automated by COMSOL and by Simcenter's BEMAO (BEM with Adaptive Order).
2. **Cap the BEM upper frequency** (e.g., solve BEM to ~2–5 kHz, where diffraction/enclosure effects dominate directivity) and **splice the analytic piston/DED model above** that, where the response is dominated by each driver's own ka beaming and is well-approximated analytically. This is the single most effective tactic for the budget.
3. **Axisymmetric (AxiBEM) shortcuts** where geometry allows (a single driver on a round baffle) — orders of magnitude cheaper.
4. **Baffle-only / reduced meshes** (fine front baffle + nearby box, coarsen the rear).
5. **Sparse frequency grid + SH interpolation** (simulate ~2 bins per octave-band, interpolate) — ~86% time savings demonstrated.
6. **Per-driver superposition**: solve each driver once at unit velocity, store its H-columns; the beamformer then combines them with filters for free (no re-solve) — exactly AKABAK's "changing lumped elements does not require re-solving" and the GLL philosophy.

## Recommendations (staged, with decision thresholds)

**Stage 0 — Prototype the toolchain on the easy case (≈1–2 weeks).** Stand up NumCalc/Mesh2HRTF (free, EUPL) with the Blender→NC.inp→NumCalc→Python path. Validate against (a) the piston-in-baffle analytic solution and (b) a sphere. Acceptance threshold: ≲1 dB magnitude and small phase error vs analytic in the valid regime; DI → 3 dB for omni-in-baffle at low ka. This de-risks everything and teaches the workflow before committing.

**Stage 1 — Single driver in the real 3D enclosure, mid-band (≈2–4 weeks).** Mesh the user's CAD, impose a rigid-piston velocity BC from the T/S-derived cone velocity, and solve BEM to ~2–5 kHz over a Lebedev/Fliege sphere. Confirm the baffle step, diffraction ripple, and edge-rounding effects appear. Decision threshold: if a single solve exceeds ~1–2 hours at your target top frequency, invoke frequency-band remeshing or lower the BEM cap.

**Stage 2 — Add the analytic HF supplement and the driver electrical chain (≈2–3 weeks).** Above the BEM cap, splice the analytic piston directivity; apply the T/S LF rolloff and the semi-inductance HF rolloff (REW/Thorborg–Futtrup or LR2 model) as a frequency-domain electrical transfer function that multiplies every direction equally (it is a driver-terminal effect). Decision threshold: if splice artifacts (magnitude/phase discontinuity at the BEM/analytic boundary) exceed ~0.5 dB or produce audible group-delay steps, widen the BEM band or blend over a full octave.

**Stage 3 — Multi-driver, full enclosure, full sphere, all drivers (≈3–6 weeks).** Solve each driver independently (unit velocity), and store per-driver complex H[freq, direction] referenced to ONE global origin. Keep wall-clock per driver under budget by combining Stage-2 hybridization with sparse-frequency + SH interpolation. Export to native HDF5/NumPy (working), `.frd` per-driver/per-angle (VituixCAD), CLF (balloon/interop), and optionally SOFA.

**Stage 4 — Hand off to the filter designer and close the loop (ongoing).** Feed the H-tensor into the beamformer (build A/R covariance matrices per Luo, or run the GLL complex-summation forward model). Validate by reproducing a known constant-directivity result — e.g., a CBT Legendre-shaded pattern, or the NextGenAudio cardioid.

**Tool decision rule:**
- Default to **NumCalc/Mesh2HRTF** (free, scriptable, proven) for the bulk solving.
- Switch to **COMSOL BEM/FEM** if you need (a) rigorous coupled lumped-T/S → acoustic driver modeling, (b) infinite-baffle setups (use FEM+PML there), or (c) structural cone-breakup via hybrid FEM-BEM — and you can tolerate the licensing/automation effort.
- Consider **AKABAK/ABEC** if you want the most loudspeaker-native LEM+BEM coupling and balloon output in one Windows package and are willing to pay/trial it.
- Keep **OpenBEM** as the transparent MATLAB AxiBEM option for axisymmetric speed-ups and verification.
- Use **MATLAB Phased Array Toolbox / Pyroomacoustics / Acoular** only on the Half-2 side (steering, room, array-processing concepts), feeding them the simulated custom element patterns — never as the radiation physics.

## Caveats
- **"Around a day" full-band figures are loosely specified.** The NextGenAudio datapoint used point-source drivers (not full vibrating-surface BCs) on an undisclosed machine and did not report mesh size, RAM, or frequency-step count. Treat it as order-of-magnitude. The exact per-frequency seconds/GB table from the NumCalc paper (Kreuzer, Pollack, Brinkmann, Majdak, *Engineering Analysis with Boundary Elements* vol. 161, pp. 157–178, 2024; arXiv:2312.00005) could not be extracted in this research and should be read directly for precise planning numbers.
- **Cone breakup is not predicted from T/S + CAD alone.** A rigid-piston (or simple parameterized) velocity BC will miss true breakup peaks/dips and their HF directivity detail. Genuine breakup requires structural FEM of the cone plus structural-acoustic coupling, which is out of scope for the "few T/S params" input. The NextGenAudio author named the simplified driver model as his toolchain's main weakness.
- **Analytic diffraction is planar-only.** Standard GTD/DED/BTM algorithms cannot represent true 3D enclosures; they are fine for validation and the planar regime but not as the primary engine for an arbitrary-3D, side/rear-driver speaker.
- **Phase-reference discipline is the highest-risk software detail.** If per-driver responses are independently minimum-phase-ified or re-zeroed, beams will mis-steer. Enforce a single global phase origin throughout.
- **Infinite-baffle BEM pitfall in COMSOL**: the Infinite Sound Hard Boundary "cannot have a hole in it," so a baffled driver must be modeled with FEM+PML, not BEM, in COMSOL.
- **Phased Array Toolbox is RF/sonar-native**: idealized far-field point elements, scalar field, no diffraction/mutual-coupling physics — useful only as a steering/visualization sandbox.
- **Spatial and frequency aliasing**: the sphere grid must over-resolve the SH order the optimizer uses; a sparse simulated frequency grid must be interpolated carefully (SH + minimum-phase domain) to avoid artifacts.
- **NumCalc practicalities**: single-threaded per instance and CPU-only — plan for many cores + abundant RAM and a job manager that launches highest-frequency (most RAM-hungry) steps first.
